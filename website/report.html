Introduction

This report details the design and implementation of a music player equalizer using MATLAB. The project aims to analyze audio signals contaminated with various types of noise, and then design and apply digital filters to enhance the audio quality. The project is divided into four main parts: signal acquisition, frequency-domain analysis, filter design, and performance evaluation.

Methodology

The methodology for this project is as follows:

  • Signal Acquisition: Three music files were recorded in different noisy environments. These audio files were then loaded into MATLAB for processing. The time-domain representation and sampling frequency of each signal were analyzed.
  • Frequency-Domain Analysis: The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) was computed for each signal to analyze its frequency content. The dominant frequency components were identified, and the magnitude spectrums were plotted to visualize the frequency distribution of the audio and the noise.
  • Filter Design: Based on the frequency analysis, appropriate digital filters were designed for each noisy audio file. The filter selection, cutoff frequencies, and other design parameters were justified.
  • Performance Evaluation: The effectiveness of the filters was evaluated both qualitatively, by listening to the filtered audio, and quantitatively, by discussing the limitations of metrics like Mean Square Error (MSE) and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) in the absence of a clean reference signal.

Design

The filter design process involved selecting an appropriate filter type and determining the correct parameters to remove the identified noise from each audio signal.

Filter Selection and Justification

The Butterworth filter was chosen for its maximally flat frequency response in the passband, which ensures that the audio signal is not distorted while the noise is being removed. The magnitude of the Butterworth filter's frequency response is given by the formula:

$$ |H(j\\Omega)| = \\frac{1}{\\sqrt{1 + (\\frac{\\Omega}{\\Omega_c})^{2N}}} $$

Where:

  • $|H(j\\Omega)|$ is the magnitude of the frequency response
  • $\\Omega$ is the frequency
  • $\\Omega_c$ is the cutoff frequency
  • $N$ is the filter order
A filter order of 6 was chosen as a compromise between a sharp cutoff and computational complexity.

Filter Implementations

  • Audio 1 (Thunder): A high-pass filter with a cutoff frequency of 100 Hz was designed to remove the low-frequency thunder rumble.
  • Audio 2 (Autumn Walk): A low-pass filter with a cutoff frequency of 4000 Hz was designed to remove the high-frequency hiss.
  • Audio 3 (Data Center): A high-pass filter with a cutoff frequency of 150 Hz was designed to remove the low-frequency hum from the data center equipment.

Results

The designed filters were applied to their respective noisy signals. The results were evaluated by comparing the frequency spectrums of the original and filtered signals, and by listening to the audio before and after filtering.

  • The high-pass filter for Audio 1 successfully attenuated the low-frequency components corresponding to the thunder noise, resulting in a clearer audio signal.
  • The low-pass filter for Audio 2 effectively removed the high-frequency hiss, making the music more pleasant to listen to.
  • The high-pass filter for Audio 3 significantly reduced the low-frequency hum, improving the overall audio quality.

The frequency response plots of the filters and the spectrum analysis of the filtered signals confirmed the successful removal of the targeted noise components.

Discussion

The qualitative evaluation of the filtered audio confirmed the effectiveness of the designed filters. However, there are some limitations to this approach. The filter design was based on a visual inspection of the frequency spectrum, which can be subjective. A more objective approach would involve using a clean reference signal to calculate metrics like MSE and SNR, but such a signal was not available.

The choice of a Butterworth filter with a fixed order is also a limitation. Different filter types or adaptive filters could potentially yield better results by adjusting to the specific characteristics of the noise.

Conclusion

This project successfully demonstrated the process of analyzing noisy audio signals and designing digital filters to improve their quality. The implemented equalizer was effective in removing various types of noise, including low-frequency rumble, high-frequency hiss, and low-frequency hum. Future improvements could include the use of adaptive filters, which would allow the equalizer to adjust to different noise environments automatically.
